Math Wizard and the Fantastic Foodtruck

For this project, i was brought in to completely redesign the UX and UI of the game after its poor performance in the alpha tests, which meant analyzing and redesigning every feature from the flow, to the creation of the UI elements, to the final implementation into Unity. In every step of the way i worked together with game designers, artists and engineers, to ensure every element of my design was coherent with the overall experience and aesthetics of the game, and that every feature was working as expected. My work is the result of that collaboration.

The main kitchen hub was one of the main focuses of my work during the project; combining what previously were 3 different scenes into one made for a more fluid and smooth experience, which let kids get right into the gameplay whenever they wanted to, solving the common complaint of the previous version being too slow to navigate and overly redundant.

One of the most impactful features i worked on was the redesign of ingredient selection screen, which was one of the most common complaints from the users on testing, mainly because of how confusing and unintuitive it was. Being a screen that always appeared before starting a dish, it created a bottleneck where users would either get frustrated before starting gameplay, or drop the game altogether after a few instances of the screen. The redesign looked to make this screen easier to read and more intuitive to interact with, as well as give better context to the kids in terms of what they are cooking. After launch it became one of the high points of the game, with kids having fun tweaking and trying different kinds of recipes.

All the iconography for the ingredient treatments (minigames to "cook" the ingredients after creating them) were made with the intention of being identifiable at first glance by color, and for ease of legibility with or without an object inside of them

The bazaar, which was a store for different hats, potions and skins for the game was another feature i was heavily involved with, as it wasn't considered in the alpha version of the game, as such, i worked on the screen and interactions from scratch, starting from the wireframes up until the final product.

The amount of information and new features added during the latter stages of production meant a complete overhaul of the FTUE process, which due to it's complexity involved organizing what information we needed to comunicate and its hierarchy; followed by the way it needed to be presented, in order to create a flow that wasn't too taxing on children. All of this process was tested with users via prototypes.

The style of the UI was made with magical, spellbook like touches to fit with the rest of the line of games, with an added spin of wooden textures to give it it's own identity.

Tangram redesign

For this project our team was tasked with redesigning the current product into a more appealing version for children, in my case this meant taking the new flows that were proposed and updating the UI accordingly to the new experience we wanted to deliver to the kids. As this was my first project at Osmo, there was a lot of learning involved, from how they managed their design pipeline, as well as understanding the experience osmo products want to deliver as a whole.

First i defined the initial flows based on the gameplay goals proposed by the game designer and initial wireframes were made

After a few iterations, i managed to reach an interface fitting to the style of the game, that adapted to every different world and took advantage of their variety to create a more appealing experience overall.

When designing the customization screen, i tried to maintain the simplicity found in the rest of the game, with a focus on usability and intuitivity, since we couldn't rely on text, with our target users leaning more on the younger side. At the same time, we tried to focus this screen on the main character, and give it a "wardrobe" feel to it that matches it's energy and uses it to our advantage.

One of the more interesting aspects of the game was the introduction of an "adaptive difficulty" system, that let the game match it's difficulty to the kids ability, creating a smoother experience for kids that weren't really sure of the correct difficulty level for them

In this case, the focus on the UI was on colorful and easy to recognize shapes, favoring rounder shapes in order to contrast with the more angular shapes of the characters and the tangrams.

Squiggle Magic

For this project we were tasked to create the special content of the main game, to be delivered monthly. However, aside from the main loop of the game (which is to make the shapes shown in screen using the physical pieces available) we were given full freedom on how we wanted to deliver the experience of the game.

Initially the game was controlled by buttons on the bottom of the screen for each "room" in the level, however after user testing i realized that kids were intuitively dragging the screen to try and move the characters, so a new iteration was made taking that into account, i however left the buttons for the kids that preferred to do so.

The design of the ui was made with the main user in mind, as such we tried to give it a more rough, and hand drawn feel, with more literal icons.

In order to better fit with the game world, we made it so each level has its particular color theme, affecting the ingame UI as well as the activity screen.

Customization was one of the new features considered for this content, as such a new screen, along with new flows had to be designed from the ground up.

Final design

Since the UI had to be on the simpler side on behalf of our target user, most of the work had to be done on designing the flows of the game, main example of this was designing the end of room flow, which had multiple triggers and would usually confuse users. The end product managed to organize all triggers in a narrative fashion in order to properly convey all the information needed.

Alvea: legend of the windwhisperers

Alvea is a game that focus on using elements often considered "Dark design", like lootboxes or other practices, for a good purpose in helping kids with chronic illnesses to adhere to their daily treatment. As my graduation project, this meant i was involved in every aspect of the production, from the initial investigation, to the creation of the game design document to the final prototype implementation, and became a good foundation of my skills later into my career.

The initial stages of design involved analyzing currently available games with similar objectives to mine, in order to determine what elements were missing that these products could be improved upon, followed by a user research process in order to determine the visual and gameplay elements my target user preferred. Once those elements were defined an initial game concept document was defined, and later refined into a game design document.

The wireframing process started with work on paper, with the main focus of defining what information was needed to show on the screen and how to organize it, which was later cleaned up on pc and refined into a final product.

The same process was used to make the ingame UI, this time using the gameplay flows defined in the game design document in order to determine the necessary information to be displayed.

Background design process; 4 different regions were conceptualized, based on different player traits recognized from the user research, from thumbnails to color.

4 characters were design in order to exemplify the style of each region, as well as a main character and enemy, both with their respective animations to be implemented into the prototype.

Finally everything was implemented into unity, and made into a prototype in order to show the core loop of the game.

About me

As a designer, i believe games to be the most pure form of experience design, which is why im passionate about making them. I enjoy the fine tuning that goes in every detail of a game in order to deliver a joyful and entertaining experience.

I graduated with a BA degree in Integral Design at the University of Valparaiso in Chile, later specializing in UX/UI Design through the Talento Digital program sponsored by Sence through Desafio Latam academy. Before starting my professional journey, i already knew i wanted to work on the Videogame industry, as a result i aquired a lot of skills and knowledge as a self-taught professional in game design and ux/ui design, experience that helped me not only get into my current position, but also made me a self sufficient, proactive, organized and thorough professional capable of adapting to different work environments. As such i value places where i can be in a constant state of learning, looking for different challenges and more ways to grow as a professional.
